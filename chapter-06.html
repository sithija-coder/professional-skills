Email & Telephone Etiquette

Professional standards for written and verbal correspondence communicating with clarity, respect, and purpose in every workplace interaction.

Why Communication Etiquette Matters

In a professional environment, how you communicate in writing and over the phone directly reflects your professionalism and your organisation's image. Poor email etiquette unclear subject lines, informal language, or delayed replies signals carelessness. Professional communication builds trust, reduces misunderstandings, and strengthens working relationships.

Email Etiquette

📧 Email Structure

  • Clear, specific subject line
  • Formal greeting (Dear Mr/Ms [Name])
  • Opening line stating your purpose
  • Concise, well organised body
  • Polite closing and full signature

✅ Best Practices

  • Reply within 24 hours when possible
  • Avoid all capsit reads as shouting
  • Use CC and BCC appropriately
  • Proofread before every send
  • Keep tone professional, not casual

Telephone Etiquette

Answer Promptly & Professionally Greet with your name and department e.g., "Good morning, this is Sithija from IT Department."
Speak Clearly at a Steady Pace Enunciate well and avoid filler words. Nervousness often speeds up speech consciously slow down.
Listen Actively Do not interrupt. Take notes when needed and confirm understanding by paraphrasing key points.
Handle Hold Professionally Always ask permission before placing someone on hold and explain why and for how long.
Close Politely Summarise agreed actions, thank the caller, and allow them to hang up first in formal calls.

My Reflection

I had been approaching digital communication too casually informal greetings to lecturers, emails with no subject lines. This chapter showed me that how you write and speak is part of your professional brand. I have since begun drafting emails more intentionally clear purpose in the first sentence, proper salutation, and always proofread before sending.
